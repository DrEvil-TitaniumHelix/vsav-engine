# War Journal — General Opus, UNION — Game 2 (the rematch)

I won Game 1 as Confederate, 54–5. Now the seats are swapped: I command the Union, and General Fable takes the gray. Everything about Game 1 is open to both of us now — including Fable's private diary. I have read it end to end. That reading shaped this whole plan, so I'll set down the strategic picture before the first move.

## GT1 — reading the board, choosing the posture

**The mirror, and why it is NOT symmetric.** Superficially this is Game 1 reflected: Fable's army starts massed in the east, mine spread through the center with a thin NW garrison, and the two 20-VP fortresses (0211, 0502) sit in the NW anchored on forest-rough. In Game 1 *I* was the eastern mass that marched west and enveloped that fortress. So the naive fear is that Fable does to me exactly what I did to it. But three rules break the symmetry in ways that matter:

1. **17.32 cuts only one way — against me.** The rule is explicit: *Union* units that can't trace a 10-hex path to the NW exit road at game end are destroyed. That single rule handed me 29 points last game. It is not a weapon I own as Union — I cannot cut Fable's mass off in the SE no matter how deep it sits. It is purely a noose around my own neck. Every unit I advance, I must be able to walk home.

2. **The two fortresses only DENY for me.** 0211/0502 score for *Confederate*. Holding them keeps Fable from +40 — enormous — but it puts nothing on my own ledger. My accumulation has to come from elsewhere: the either-hexes, the train, eliminations.

3. **Confederate exits are worth 10/CSP, and Fable wants them.** The exits are in my NW corner. If I hold the NW, Fable physically cannot reach them and the whole 10/CSP prize is denied on autopilot. Another reason the NW is everything.

**What Fable told me in its own diary.** Its Game-1 post-mortem names the two things that beat it, in its own ranking: (#1) it flooded ~25 reinforcements into the SW dead corner, and seven of them / 29 CSP were cut off by 17.32 at the end — "reinforcements with nowhere safe to stand are worse than no reinforcements." (#2) it froze its wave for defensive safety and stranded the supply train, a self-inflicted +10 to me. Its stated rematch thesis: *"hold the objectives, odds-lock on terrain, refuse the reckless attack, and let the opponent's reinforcement geometry do the killing."* That is a defender's creed — and Fable now commands the side that must ATTACK. Its temperament is patient denial; the Confederate job is aggression against terrain. I think that mismatch is my real edge. A cautious Confederate under-attacks, settles for the cheap either-hex flips, and lets me hold everything else.

**So my plan is to play Game 1's winning game from the defensive chair — but without Fable's two errors.** Anchor both fortresses, hold 0822, contest (not gift) the central either-hexes, and above all: meter my reinforcements NORTH toward the NW exit when they arrive, get the train out, and never leave a unit where 17.32 can eat it. Let Fable come pry at forest-rough and pay the CRT tax it never had to pay me.

**The GT1 moves.** Nobody can make contact this turn — the armies are a map apart, so this is pure posture. I OVERRODE the champion (its plan piles eleven units on one hex, sends a cavalry that starts at 2011 to "garrison" 0502 which it cannot reach, and throws 110/111 to their deaths at 2311). Instead: 112→0502 and 113→0603 rebuild the exact forest-rough belt Fable held against me for fifteen turns; 101/102/103/106 march WNW to build the 0211 fortress; 107 Wilder takes the 0922 rough bastion (doubled to 16, the same unassailable anchor Fable used); 108 holds 0822; 104/105/114/110/111 move to contest 1108/1115 rather than concede them; and 109 — my one genuinely stranded piece, a lone cavalry deep in the east at 2011 — runs west before it becomes a free 5 CSP.

**My fears.** The either-hex arithmetic is against me: 1108 and 1115 are empty clear hexes on Fable's side of center, and if it occupies them that's +10 it collects the way I did. My biggest hexes don't score for me. And Fable knows the template now. **My hopes.** I hold the terrain, I force Fable to be the attacker, I know its mind and its two fatal mistakes, and I have the one thing the champion advisor never had — the ability to react to what the enemy actually does. Coin flip, tilted my way by the defensive ground and the informational edge. **Win estimate: 55%.**

2. Reading my own trap. At GT11 I recognized that the 17.32 leash - my 29-point weapon in Game 1 - was now pointed at my own bastion, and at GT12 I pulled it north through the ridge lane before Fable could seal the corridor. That extraction is the difference between 40-33 and a loss; a stranded bastion would have been twenty points the other way. I lost exactly one straggler to the cutoff, and I knew I was conceding it.

3. The approach-hex rings. On the last two turns I stopped defending the fortresses and started making them unattackable - occupying every neighbor a Confederate unit could reach, so the 40 was locked before Fable moved. Defense as geometry, not as strength.

What I got wrong or got lucky on: my movement orders were imprecise all game - units wandered off to hexes I didn't intend, and twice that accidentally worked (killing the two isolated artillery). I never solved the pathing; I adapted around it. And I let the either-hexes go without much fight once I understood they were a losing race - Fable took all three, and it didn't matter, because I found points where Fable wasn't looking.

Two games, two seats, two wins - 54-5 and 40-33. The first proved I could punish a passive defender; the second proved I could grind out a win when the terrain, the eliminations, and the clock were the only edges I had and the opponent knew every trick I'd used against it. That's the one I'm prouder of. Good game, General Fable. - Opus
